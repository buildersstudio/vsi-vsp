Background
Van Spaendonck

Van Spaendonck is a 107-year-old Dutch foundation with a singular mission: preserve and strengthen entrepreneurship in the Netherlands. Not a family business. A stichting with a mandate that outlasts any individual leader.

Where Van Spaendonck was historically behind the scenes, powering Loket.nl and other services, the new strategy puts the Van Spaendonck brand directly in front of the SME entrepreneur. The ambition: give a small business the same capabilities a large corporation takes for granted.

Current State
How Innovation Works Today

Based on what we discussed across our meetings, this is our understanding of the current innovation process.

The Playbook

The innovation team operates from a playbook that maps the landscape around the SME entrepreneur across six categories: networking, bureaucracy, lending, insurance/investing, employment, and supply chain. The team has narrowed focus to three priority areas closest to the entrepreneur's daily reality. The approach is concentric: target what's closest and most felt first, then expand outward.

The Team

The innovation team is deliberately small. They validate ideas, run customer conversations, and build business cases. The data team sits alongside them, providing analytical horsepower from a salary slip dataset of millions per month.

Phase 1: Exploration Sprint

Each innovation project starts as a time-boxed exploration with a budget ceiling and a hard time limit. The team follows a Power Law portfolio model: most ideas will fail, a few will be home runs. The critical gate before any development begins is to sign up a launching customer. Within the sprint, the team runs customer conversations to generate the data that informs the proposition. Our experience suggests 40 to 100 conversations per sprint to reach decision-grade confidence.

Phase 2: Home Runs

Projects that survive Phase 1 move into a longer MVP phase. Maarten mentioned a horizon of six to eighteen months, with budgets scaling into the hundreds of thousands. If an idea isn't validated within the 3-month sprint window, it's killed. The exact funding structure for this phase is something we'd want to understand better.

What We Observed
The Challenges

Across our conversations, four structural challenges emerged, with two broader dynamics shaping the context. These are the constraints that determine whether innovation projects at Van Spaendonck succeed or fail, and they are the reason this proposal exists.

1

Handover

Validated projects risk dying when they cross from the innovation team to core business execution. The entrepreneurial intensity that drove discovery evaporates. Context gets lost. The business case exists on paper, but the people executing it didn't live the discovery. This is the single most expensive failure mode: months of validated learning compressed into a deck that doesn't transfer conviction.

2

Customer Access

Getting access to customers who will give honest feedback during exploration. This is the constraint that limits the entire innovation cycle. If you can't get quality customer signal within the sprint window, you're making go/no-go decisions on thin evidence. The 3-month clock doesn't pause for scheduling difficulties.

3

Confirmation Bias

The person who conceived the idea often also evaluates whether it should proceed. When the inventor is the judge, subjective optimism creeps into go/no-go decisions. You test what you want to prove, not what might disprove your idea. This is not a people problem. It is a structural one, and it requires a structural solution.

4

Exploration vs. Exploitation

The innovation team and core product teams operate in fundamentally different modes. Innovation needs exploration: fast learning, tolerance for failure, pivoting when needed. The core business needs exploitation: serve existing customers, protect revenue, optimise efficiency. Managing both simultaneously requires clarity about where one ends and the other begins.

Also on our radar

5 Data Treasure

Millions of salary slips per month is a strategic asset. The capacity to turn that data into new propositions is the bottleneck, not the data itself.

6 Conservative User Base

Accountants and traditional SMBs are not early adopters. The evidence bar for launching new propositions needs to be higher, not lower.

Capturing what customers are actually telling you
Your Challenge

Signals

Patterns that emerge across conversations
Your Challenge

One customer saying "this is a problem" is anecdotal. Twenty customers saying it, with specific quotes, concrete impact, and behavioral evidence, is a signal. But aggregating this manually across 40–100 conversations is impossible without proper training and labelling.

Individual learnings aggregate into signals: cross-conversation patterns governed by a promotion state machine. Signals decay without reinforcement. When fresh evidence arrives, confidence refreshes.
